Not All Locations
Are Equal

You have a territory. The question isn't whether you're covering it. It's whether you're managing it — and those are two very different things.

The Concept

If you asked most recruiters how they decide where to go on any given day, the answer would involve some combination of the schedule, the donation calendar, and a general sense of which locations need attention. That's not a strategy. That's a rotation.

A territory isn't a list of places to visit. It's a portfolio — and like any portfolio, some positions are performing, some have potential, and some are quietly costing you more than they're returning. The recruiter who manages their territory intentionally will always outperform the one who just covers it.

The shift is simple in concept and harder in practice: before you plan your week, ask yourself what category each location falls into — and let that drive where your time goes.

A Patricia Moment
Patricia visits every location in alphabetical order.

Patricia does not have anchor locations. Patricia does not have developing locations. Patricia has a list and she works the list. She starts at the top and she works down. When she gets to the bottom she starts again at the top.

Patricia's best location gets the same amount of attention as her worst one. Her best location has 22 active donors and is consistently producing. Her worst location has 2 active donors and has had 2 active donors for four months. Patricia visited both of them last Tuesday. Patricia considers this fair.

Patricia's worst location also starts with the letter A. Patricia has been there a lot.

The Three Categories

Every location in your territory falls into one of three categories. Knowing which is which changes everything about how you allocate your time.

Anchor
Strong. Consistent. You know it cold.

This location produces reliably. You know the staff, they know you, the numbers move when you're there. Your job here is to protect this status — not take it for granted and not neglect it because it seems fine. Anchors can erode quietly when recruiters stop showing up consistently.

Developing
Real potential. Needs intentional work.

This location has the ingredients — dog traffic, willing staff, engaged owners — but the numbers don't reflect it yet. The gap is almost always effort and consistency. Developing locations need more of your visits, not fewer. You're building something here and it requires deliberate attention to move it toward anchor status.

Evaluate
You keep going. Results don't justify it.

This one is hard to admit. You've been here multiple times. The numbers don't move. The staff isn't engaged, the dog traffic is thin, or the logistics make every visit more expensive than it should be. Evaluate doesn't mean abandon — it means honestly assess whether the next six visits will produce something different, and if the answer is probably not, redirect that time.

A location with 3 donors that you've visited 12 times isn't a bad location. It's a developing location that's been treated like an anchor — and visited, not worked.

What This Looks Like in Practice

Consider two locations that both exist in the same territory. One runs consistently — it's a Monday staple, the team knows exactly how many donors to expect, and it's the kind of day that anchors the week. The other location has been on the schedule for months, has a handful of active donors, and gets visits regularly — but the numbers have barely moved because those visits are happening without a deliberate plan to grow it.

Strategic Thinking in Action
Knowing when to press and when to pivot.

A recruiter had a cancellation midweek. Instead of treating it as a lost day, she called a location she'd been meaning to develop and asked if she could come do a recruiting event that evening. The staff said yes. She got a list of 25 dogs, worked the lobby, and left with meaningful conversations and new names to follow up on.

That pivot only happened because she knew her territory well enough to know which location had untapped potential and was ready for a push. You can only pivot intelligently to a location you've already been thinking about strategically.

The Logistics Layer
Know Before You Go

Part of location strategy is knowing the logistics of each location before you show up — and letting that inform when you go, not just whether you go.

Is it an indoor or outdoor pickup location? On a rainy day, an outdoor pickup location is an evaluate decision for that day regardless of its normal category. Does it run heavy on Tuesdays and light on Fridays? Showing up on the wrong day at a good location produces the same result as showing up at the wrong location.

Who is the actual decision-maker vs. who answers the phone? Who can pull you a list vs. who will just say "I don't know"? The logistics knowledge is part of what makes a location an anchor — you know how to work it, not just that it exists.
