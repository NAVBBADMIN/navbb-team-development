What Are You Actually
Looking At?

Showing up is not the same as paying attention. Critical thinking in the field starts before you get in the car.

The Concept

There's a version of field recruiting that looks like work but isn't producing anything. You drive to the locations, you talk to the staff, you smile at the dogs. You check the box. You go home. And somehow the numbers don't move.

The gap isn't effort. It's observation. Most people see what's in front of them. The best recruiters are processing it — asking themselves why this dog is here, what this owner's schedule probably looks like, what this location's pattern has been over the last three weeks, and what one question would tell them the most in the least amount of time.

That's critical thinking in the field. Not a philosophy class. Not a personality trait. A habit — and one that can be built deliberately.

A Patricia Moment
Patricia arrives at a daycare she has visited fourteen times.

She walks in. She sees dogs. She counts them. She attempts to recruit the ones that are there. Three say no. Two are ineligible. She drives home and marks the visit complete.

Patricia has never once asked herself why this location seems to run light on Tuesdays. She has never noticed that the owner's golden retriever has been there every single visit and has never been pitched. She has never asked a staff member what the busiest day usually looks like.

Patricia is very thorough about doing the same thing fourteen times in a row and expecting a different result. Patricia considers this persistence.

What Good Looks Like

Earlier this year, a recruiter on the team hit a wall. Low numbers, lots of drives, nothing changing. She decided to spend one Friday afternoon differently — not recruiting, just watching and asking.

Critical Thinking in Action
The Friday that changed how she works every week.

She didn't go in with a pitch. She went in with questions. What days are you usually busiest? Do you have regulars — dogs that come on a schedule? What's the mix usually like between daycare and boarding?

By the end of that afternoon she had something she'd never had before — a mental model of each location. Not just "this place has dogs sometimes" but a picture of which dogs, which days, which owners were worth calling by name.

Her numbers changed the following week. Not because she worked harder. Because she finally knew what she was actually looking at.

The Framework

Critical thinking in field recruiting breaks down into three habits. None of them require extra time — they require redirecting attention you're already spending.

01
Ask one more question

Before you leave any location, ask one question you didn't come in planning to ask. Not about the dogs — about the patterns. "What's your busiest day?" "Do you have regulars?" "Has anyone ever donated before?" One question compounds over time.

02
Name your assumptions

Before you arrive anywhere, say out loud what you expect to find. Then check it. This is the Ivy Lee principle applied to field work — clarity before action. You can't spot a wrong assumption if you never named it in the first place.

03
Notice the pattern, not just the visit

One visit is a data point. Three visits is a pattern. Five visits is a relationship. If you've been somewhere more than twice and you still can't predict what you'll find when you arrive, you haven't been paying attention — you've been showing up.

Showing up and paying attention are not the same thing. One of them fills your calendar. The other one fills your pipeline.

The Ivy Lee Connection

Casey mentioned the Ivy Lee method in her notes on what's worked in past book club discussions. Here's why it's relevant here: Ivy Lee's core idea wasn't about working harder — it was about deciding in advance what actually matters before you start moving. He told Charles Schwab to write down six things the night before and work them in order. The insight wasn't the list. It was the act of deciding before the chaos of the day took over.

Applied to field recruiting: the recruiter who thinks before she drives — who asks "what do I actually expect to find, and what one question will tell me the most" — is doing Ivy Lee in the field. The recruiter who just shows up is letting the day decide for her.

Patricia does not write anything down the night before. Patricia considers this staying flexible.
