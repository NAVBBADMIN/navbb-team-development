The Recruiter
They Call

Anyone can show up to a location. The recruiter who gets called back is the one who made showing up mean something.

The Concept

There are two kinds of recruiters in any territory. The first kind shows up, pitches dogs, and leaves. They work hard. They drive a lot. They're consistent in the sense that they consistently do the same thing every visit. Their numbers are okay.

The second kind does something different. They learn names. They follow up on things that don't directly benefit them. They show up to a donor event at 4:30pm and introduce themselves to every owner in the room like they actually want to be there. Over time, the staff at a location starts to look forward to seeing them. And eventually — the owners start calling them.

That's the difference between working a territory and owning one. It's not talent. It's not charisma. It's three habits applied consistently over time.

A Patricia Moment
Patricia has a lanyard. Patricia considers herself a relationship builder.

Patricia has visited the same six locations for eight months. She does not know the name of a single staff member at any of them. She knows the dogs' names. She does not know the people who bring those dogs in every week.

Patricia sent a follow-up email once in 2023. She still mentions this. Patricia considers her lanyard to be professional. Patricia's lanyard has her name spelled wrong and she has not noticed.

The Three Habits
01
Know the names

Staff names. Owner names. The name of the regular's dog. This isn't small talk — it's infrastructure. Every name you know is a relationship node. Every name you don't know is a missed connection that compounds over time into being just another face that comes through.

02
Follow up on things that don't benefit you immediately

If a staff member mentioned their dog was sick last visit — ask about it this visit. If an owner said they might be interested — send a one-line message two weeks later with no pitch. This is how trust is built. It costs almost nothing and almost nobody does it.

03
Show up the same way every time

Consistency is underrated as a professional tool. When a location can predict how you'll behave — enthusiastic, prepared, easy to work with — they start to plan around you. You become a known quantity in the best possible way. Patricia is inconsistent in every dimension. The staff at her top location once forgot her name.

Relationships are not built in visits. They're built in the gaps between visits — in the follow-up, the memory, and the consistency.

Daytime vs. Donor Events

Professionalism looks different at a daytime partner visit than it does at a donor event. Both matter. Neither is harder — they just require different things.

Donor Events · 4:00 – 6:30 PM

The evening shift is its own skill set.

At a donor event you're not pitching a dog — you're meeting the person who loves that dog. The owner standing across from you at 5pm has just come from work, brought their dog in for something that makes them feel good, and is deciding in about 90 seconds whether they trust you enough to have a real conversation. Your energy, your openness, and whether you seem like you actually want to be there will do more work in those 90 seconds than your pitch will do in five minutes. Show up ready. Not just present — ready.

What Good Looks Like
She wasn't the most experienced recruiter in the territory. She was the most consistent one.

There's a recruiter who inherited a territory with four locations that had seen three different faces in two years. Low trust, low numbers, lots of skepticism from staff.

She didn't try to fix it all at once. She picked one thing: she learned every staff member's name at every location within her first month. She wrote them down after every visit. She used them every time she walked in. Within six weeks, staff members were texting her when a new regular came in that looked like a good candidate.

That's what consistent professionalism compounds into. Not overnight. Not even in a month. But faster than you'd think.
