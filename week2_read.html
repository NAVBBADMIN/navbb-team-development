Work Smarter,
Not Longer

Time management isn't about doing more. It's about knowing which things are actually worth doing — and doing those first.

The Concept

Here's the thing about a busy day in the field: busy and productive are not the same thing. You can drive to six locations, talk to a dozen people, and come home with nothing to show for it — not because you didn't work hard, but because the six locations weren't the right six locations at the right time.

Time management in recruiting isn't a calendar problem. It's an energy management problem. Your time is fixed. Your energy is fixed. The question is where you point both of them — and whether you're making that call intentionally or just reacting to whatever's in front of you.

A Patricia Moment
Patricia has a system. Several, actually.

Patricia has a color-coded weekly planner, a digital calendar with time blocks, a sticky note system, a whiteboard with zones, and 47 browser tabs she considers "active research." Patricia spent Tuesday driving 40 minutes to a daycare that had zero dogs in because she didn't call ahead. She spent Wednesday calling locations she'd already called Monday.

Patricia is very busy. Patricia has not recruited a single new dog this week.

Having a system is not the same as using your time well. The system only works if it answers one question first: is this the right thing to be doing right now?

What Good Looks Like

Earlier this summer, a recruiter on the team hit a wall — low daycare numbers, lots of drives, not much to show. Instead of doing the same thing again and hoping for different results, she changed her approach entirely.

A Real-World Example
The Friday Road Trip That Changed the Approach

Frustrated with the numbers, she spent one Friday afternoon hitting five locations in one trip — not to recruit on the spot, but to gather intelligence. At each stop: who's boarding vs. in daycare? Do they come on a schedule or drop in? Get names. Get patterns.

The result: instead of calling the next week and asking blindly "do you have dogs in daycare today?" — she had a list of specific dogs to ask for by name. Dogs with known schedules. Leads she could work, not cold calls into the void.

She didn't work more hours. She worked the same hours smarter — by using one afternoon to build the intelligence that made every future call more efficient.

The Framework

There are a lot of time management frameworks out there. Most of them are built for office workers with inboxes. Here's one that actually maps to field recruiting — three questions, asked in order, before you plan your day.

01
Where is the energy?

Before you go anywhere, find out what's actually available. Call ahead. Check patterns. A daycare with twelve borders and two daycare dogs isn't worth the same drive as one with twenty in daycare. Know before you go.

02
What's the best use of a drive?

If you're going somewhere with low availability, can you make it worth the trip anyway? Leads, love notes, relationship maintenance, intel on new locations nearby. A drive should never be zero-return — build something even when you can't collect something.

03
What does tomorrow need today?

The best time managers in field roles build tomorrow's day while they're still in today's. Five minutes before you leave for the day — who are you calling first tomorrow? What do you already know about their schedule? Don't start the next day from zero.

Busy is easy. Effective is a choice you make before you get in the car.

Sound Familiar?
Scenario

A recruiter starts Monday with no specific plan — just a mental list of locations to hit. She goes to her first stop at 9am: four dogs in daycare, none eligible. Second stop at 10:30: closed for a staff training day she didn't know about. Third stop at noon: five eligible dogs, but she's already running late and only has twenty minutes before she needs to leave for an afternoon appointment.

By 3pm she's driven 60 miles and has one solid lead. Not because she didn't work — she drove all day. But because the day was reactive instead of planned. The 60 miles happened to her rather than being chosen by her.

The next Friday she calls ahead before leaving the house. She maps three stops in the same corridor where the numbers look promising. She has names to ask for at two of them. She's home by 2pm with four solid leads and a full list for next week's calls.

Same recruiter. Same territory. Different result — because she planned the day instead of reacting to it.
