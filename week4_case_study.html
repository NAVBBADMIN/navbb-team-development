The Call She Didn't Have to Make

~8 min · Click through each scene, then answer one question.
Scene 1 of 6
Four Months Ago

A donor event. A brief conversation. Nothing that felt significant.

It was a Thursday evening event, running from 4:30 to 6:30. A recruiter was working the room — introducing herself to owners, talking about the program, collecting intake forms. Standard evening.

At about 5:15 she ended up talking to the owner of a three-year-old lab mix named Biscuit. The conversation ran maybe eight minutes. The owner was interested but not ready to commit. She had questions about the process, the frequency, whether her dog would be okay. The recruiter answered them honestly, including the parts that weren't a guaranteed yes.

At the end she handed her a card and said: "Take your time. There's no rush. If you want to ask more questions before deciding, just reach out." And then she moved on.

Two Weeks Later

She followed up. Once. Without a pitch.

She sent a single message — not a form email, not a pitch, not a link to a scheduling page. Just a one-line note: "Hey — hope Biscuit is doing well. No pressure at all, just wanted to make sure you had what you needed if you had any more questions."

The owner replied with a few more questions. The recruiter answered them. No close attempt. No urgency. Just answers.

Patricia's Version

Patricia also attended a donor event that month. Patricia has not followed up with a single owner from that event. Patricia meant to. Patricia is very busy. Patricia considers the event itself to have been the follow-up.

The Next Two Months

Nothing. And that was fine.

She didn't hear from the owner. She didn't follow up again. She didn't reach back out after a month with another nudge. She let it sit.

This is the part that most recruiters get wrong. The impulse is to follow up again — to stay top of mind, to push a little. But there's a version of follow-up that tips into pressure, and owners can feel the difference. She had answered the questions. She had been easy to reach. She had made it clear there was no rush. That was enough.

0 additional follow-up attempts after the initial message. She trusted the relationship she'd already built to do the work.
Four Months Later — A Tuesday Afternoon

Her phone rang. It was the owner.

The owner had thought about it. She'd talked to a friend who also donated. She'd decided she wanted to bring Biscuit in — and she also wanted to bring in her sister's golden retriever, who she thought might be a good candidate.

That's two dogs. Two completed intakes. Scheduled on the same day.

The recruiter didn't make this happen with a script or a pitch. She made it happen by being someone worth calling four months later. That's professionalism working as a competitive advantage.

What Built the Call

It wasn't one thing. It was a sequence of small ones.

Donor Event
Honest conversation, no pressure close. She answered the hard questions including the ones without perfect answers.
Two Weeks Out
One personal follow-up, no pitch. She checked in on Biscuit by name. She didn't ask for a decision.
Months of Silence
She didn't push. She trusted that she'd done enough to be worth calling if the owner was ready.
Result
Inbound call. Two donors. No chase.
Your Turn

Is there an owner in your territory who went quiet after showing initial interest?

Not someone who said no — someone who was warm and then just... didn't move forward. What did your follow-up look like? Was it personal or transactional? Did you push or did you leave space? What would you do differently now?
