Before You Start
Meet Patricia

Patricia is a fictional NAVBB opossum who shows up across every chapter as the cautionary example — the "what not to do" stand-in, exaggerated for effect. She's not based on anyone. She's just an opossum with consistently bad instincts, used to make the serious points land a little lighter.

The Concept

Nobody wakes up and decides to drop the ball. Nobody's thinking "you know what would be great today? Letting a donation day detail slip through the cracks." It's never malicious. It's almost always fuzzy ownership — a thing existed in the space between two or three people, everyone assumed someone else had eyes on it, and then it just didn't get done.

That gap — between "this is technically someone's job" and "I am the person making sure this happens" — is where accountability actually lives. It's a muscle, not a personality trait. Some people are wired for it. The rest of us have to build it on purpose, the same way you build a habit of not leaving your blinker on for eleven miles.

A Patricia Moment
Patricia notices the printer is out of toner.

Patricia does not order toner. Patricia does not tell anyone. Patricia, an opossum, physically lies down next to the printer and waits for the situation to resolve itself through sheer stillness.

It does not resolve itself. It is still happening. The printer is now a shrine.

The Idea

Most accountability advice tries to scare you into it. We're skipping that. Here's the useful version, borrowed from the "Extreme Ownership" school of leadership thinking: the person who notices the problem is, functionally, the person responsible for it — not because it's fair, but because someone has to be, and noticing first is as good a tiebreaker as any.

This isn't about blame. It's about being the kind of teammate people don't have to double-check. That reputation — "if I hand this to them, it's handled" — is worth more than almost anything else you can build at work. It's the professional equivalent of being the friend who actually shows up with the U-Haul.

"Not my job" might be technically true and still be the wrong thing to say out loud.

Sound Familiar?
Scenario

A donor reschedule comes in late Thursday for a Saturday collection. The email lands in a shared inbox. Three people see it pass through their notifications over the next two days. Each one assumes someone else grabbed it — surely someone did, right?

Saturday morning, the team shows up expecting a donor who isn't coming, and nobody arranged a replacement slot. Nobody lied. Nobody ignored it on purpose. It just belonged to everyone, which means it belonged to Patricia, which means it belonged to absolutely nobody at all.

This is the pattern almost every "how did this happen" moment traces back to. Not a skills gap. An ownership gap. A Patricia-shaped hole where a decision should've been.
