Two Territories, Two Approaches

Scene 1 of 6
Same Region. Same Pool of Locations.

Two recruiters. Overlapping territories. Very different results.

Recruiter A and Recruiter B both work in the same general region. They have access to many of the same daycares, similar dog populations, and similar schedule structures. Over a six-week period, their numbers diverge significantly — not because one works harder than the other, but because of how each one thinks about their territory.

Recruiter A visits 12 locations a week. Recruiter B visits 6. Recruiter B's sign-up rate is nearly double.

Recruiter A — The Coverage Approach

Recruiter A is everywhere. And nowhere deeply.

Recruiter A's weekly schedule looks impressive on paper. Twelve locations. Every day packed. She rarely misses a visit and she can tell you how many dogs were at each location on any given Tuesday.

12 locations visited per week · average 45 minutes per location · 4 locations with fewer than 5 active donors that have had fewer than 5 active donors for 3+ months · 0 locations she'd describe as anchors

When a location has a bad week she moves on. When a location has a good week she comes back the same way she always does. She covers her territory. She does not manage it.

Recruiter B — The Portfolio Approach

Recruiter B knows exactly what she's working with.

Before Recruiter B plans her week, she thinks about three categories. She has three anchor locations — places she knows cold, where the staff knows her, and where she can reliably produce. She has two developing locations she is deliberately building toward anchor status. And she has one location she is actively evaluating — deciding whether the next four visits will produce something different.

Week Three — A Cancellation

A location cancels. Watch how each recruiter responds.

On a Wednesday afternoon, a location calls to cancel its recruiting visit. Recruiter A marks the visit as canceled, adjusts the schedule, and calls it an early day. She had nothing else planned for that slot — she hadn't been thinking strategically enough about her territory to know immediately where to redirect the time.

Recruiter B gets the same cancellation. She knows exactly what to do. She calls a developing location — one she's been meaning to give a push — and asks if she can come for an impromptu recruiting event that evening. The staff says yes. She works a list of dogs, has real conversations, and leaves with four names to follow up on and one sign-up on the spot.

You can only pivot intelligently to a location you've already been thinking about strategically. The pivot isn't spontaneous. It's the result of knowing your territory well enough to know where the opportunity is before you need it.

Week Six — The Numbers

Same region. Same time period. Very different results.

Recruiter A — 12 Locations

31 new sign-ups over six weeks. Four locations stuck at under 5 donors for the entire period. One location she visited 8 times that produced 1 donor. No sense of which locations are growing and which are stalled.

Recruiter B — 6 Locations

47 new sign-ups over six weeks. Both developing locations moved up in donor count. Evaluate location gets one more month. Three anchors maintained and deepened. One opportunistic pivot that produced 4 follow-up conversations and 1 sign-up.

Recruiter A worked harder. Recruiter B worked smarter. The difference wasn't talent or personality — it was the decision, made before the week started, about what each location was worth and what it needed.

Your Turn

Do you have anchor locations — or do you have a list?

Think about your territory right now. Can you name three locations you'd consider anchors — places where you know the staff, the numbers move consistently, and you'd put in the top tier of your portfolio? If you can, what makes them anchors? If you can't name three, what's missing from the ones that are closest?
